Abstract
This chapter addresses Megrelian, a Kartvelian (South Caucasian) language spoken by
Megrelians, a subethnic group compactly residing in one of the western provinces of Georgia,
Samegrelo. A language of informal communication, Megrelian has been subject to linguistic
research both in Georgia and beyond for more than 200 years now.
Backed by the existing literature on the language, most of which has been published in
Georgian, this sketch provides an account of essential features of Megrelian phonology,
grammar, and lexicon, including such typologically renowned properties of Megrelian as the
elaborate system of preverbs and innovative and extremely specific case-marking alignment
that not only features ergative stimuli of affective verbs, but can also license this case to
adverbs as well. Furthermore, new insights are proposed for such domains of linguistic
structure as the language’s case system, grades of comparison, expression of spatial deixis by
pronominal expressions, verbal aspect, and evidentiality; some of these statements are based
on the data from the author’s long-term fieldwork and are now being introduced to linguistic
discourse.
Keywords

12.1. The Language and Its Speakers
Megrelian (autoglossonym margaluri nina, Georgian glossonym megruli ena; ISO 639-3
name ‘xmf’, Glottolog name ‘ming1252’), also known as Colchian (Colchidian) or Iverian, is
an unwritten language spoken by Megrelians, a subethnic group of Georgians compactly
residing in Western Georgia, in the historical area of Samegrelo (Megrelian Samargalo). The
Megrelian-speaking community also used to inhabit Abkhazia before the war in 1992–1993,
with most of that poplulation now living as displaced persons in Tbilisi and other cities of
Georgia; however, some of them have remained in the Gali and T’q’varčeli districts in the
southeast of breakaway Abkhazia.1
Megrelian is mutually unintelligible with the dominant language of the area, Georgian,
not to mention the family outlier, Svan. Some mutual intelligibility connects Megrelian and
northern varieties of Laz spoken in Sarpi (Georgia) and, to a lesser extent, Hopa, a bordering
territory in Turkey. Native speakers of other dialects of Laz do not understand Megrelian.
Together with its closest sister language, Laz, Megrelian constitutes the Zan subgroup of
the Kartvelian family.2
Megrelian has two major dialects: Samurzaq’ano-Zugdidian in the northwest and
Senak’ian in the southeast of the Megrelian-speaking area. These primarily differ in phonetics
and lexicon; morphological differences are less pronounced. The Samurzaq’ano-Zugdidian

Megrelian is locally spoken in the historical region of Samegrelo, enclosed between the
Black Sea and the Rioni, Cxenisc’q’ali, and Enguri rivers; the language thus borders Svan in
the north and Georgian in the east (the historical regions of Imereti and, to a lesser extent,
Lečxumi) and in the south (Guria).
According to the 2014 census,3 the population of Samegrelo is over 300 thousand people.
With the only exception of the harbor town of Poti, which constitutes a rather colorful
mosaic both ethnically and linguistically, the rest of the population of Samegrelo is rather
homogeneous both in terms of self-identity and language, the overwhelming majority either
speaking Megrelian as their mother tongue (next to Georgian) or at least having a good
command of it.
There are no exact figures with respect to the Gali and T’q’varčeli minorities in
Abkhazia, but they probably number within the range of ten thousand people; the locals tend
to refer to themselves as Georgians and mainly speak Megrelian.
Megrelians represent the northern branch of the Zan linguistic area, which is split in two
by the Georgian-speaking regions (viz., Guria and Ač’ara), with their Laz kin remaining in the
south. Glotto-chronological research evidence suggests that Zan must have developed from
western Kartvelian dialects that had spread within what is now Western Georgia in the second
millennium BC (Klimov 1998a: xii), see Chapter 11. The Megrelian language community
identifies with Georgian culture and history, but this identification also has local overtones.
Megrelians are Orthodox Christians and belong to the Georgian Orthodox Church,
Georgia’s dominant religious institution. The language of liturgy is exclusively Georgian.

As was mentioned above, Megrelian is an unwritten language used in everyday life and
for non-official communication in Samegrelo. Other spheres of use include trade at local
markets; smaller shops welcome the language, too, as is also the case with all other
institutions in Samegrelo where informal communication is preferred. In all other instances,
Georgian is used. Megrelian is not taught at school, although universities in Tbilisi and
Kutaisi offer practical courses in the language.
Nearly all Megrelians are native speakers of Georgian; however, for the most part, their
Georgian has a distinctive Megrelian accent and specific grammatical features. In the districts
of Gali and T’q’varčeli, Georgian partially yields its lingua franca status to Russian.
Megrelian-Abkhazian bilingualism has also been attested.
In the 1930s, attempts were made to grant Megrelian the status of a written language
(which subsequently led to the production of periodical newspapers, see section 12.2.2).
These efforts came to naught, and the newspapers ceased publication on orders from Moscow.
Currently, there are no mass media functioning in Megrelian within the Republic of Georgia,
but in Gali, Abkhazian authorities have been publishing a Megrelian bimonthly newspaper
called “Gal” and are currently launching a Megrelian TV channel, “Gal-TV.
”4 These
developments are viewed negatively in Georgia, including by the population of Samegrelo,
for they are perceived as another step toward conflict escalation and the rise of the anti-
Georgian sentiment in the region.
Advocates of the Megrelian language and culture, many of them foreigners, have argued
that Megrelian has the potential to develop into a literary language. They base upon a body of
literature translated into Megrelian (for the most part from Georgian, including several
versions of Šota Rustaveli’s nation-wide celebrated “Knight in the Panther’s Skin”) and on

some work by contemporary writers and poets. Most Megrelians treat books in their language
more as a decoration for their homes than as something they would ever think of actually
reading. Although Klimov (1998b, p. 52) claims that Megrelian serves as a means of informal
correspondence,5 Megrelians often experience difficulties reading and writing in their
language.6
Megrelian is not endangered in the sense of the number of speakers, but it is substantially
affected by Georgian and not spoken monolingually. These factors point to the urgency of
documenting this language.
Although Megrelians are often perceived as descendants of the ancient Kingdom of
Colchis (Egrisi), the endonym megrel- ‘Megrelian’ was first mentioned in Prince Stepanoz’
inscription in Old Georgian found in the At’eni Sioni church and dated back to 735 A.D. by
Guram Abramišvili (1977). The text features a genitive NP k~ta da meg~rlta eris that is to be
interpreted as kartvelta da megrelta eris ‘of the people of Kartli and Egrisi’. There are also
numerous mentions in such historical sources as “Kartlis Cxovreba” (“The Georgian
Chronicles”), compiled between the 9th and 14th centuries A.D.

12.2 State of Research
Megrelian inflectional and derivational morphology is relatively well studied. On the other
hand, such linguistic domains as (suprasegmental) phonetics, syntax, and discourse structure
still await in-depth research.
12.2.1 Brief History of Research
Megrelian data were mentioned for the first time in Güldenstädt’s cross-Caucasian
dictionaries in the appendix to his Caucasian travel report (1791), in the form of a word list
featuring ca. 250 lexical items. The first grammatical information on Megrelian appeared in
Rosen’s (1846) comparative essay on Ossetian, Megrelian, Svan, and Abkhaz, where ten
pages were devoted to Megrelian nouns, pronouns, and verbs, with several declension and
conjugation samples provided. The foundation of linguistic descriptions was completed by
Alexandre Cagareli (Tsagareli) who published Megrelian texts accompanied by Russian
translations and an essay on Megrelian segmental phonetics (Tsagareli, 1880a, 1880b).
12.2.2 Grammatical Descriptions and Dictionaries
Several monographs address the language in Russian (Q’ipšiʒe, 1914; Tsagareli, 1880b) and
Georgian (Čikobava, 1936; K’art’ozia, Gersamia, Lomia, & Cxadaia, 2011; Kiria, Ezugbaia,
Memišiši, & Chukhua, 2015); there is also an 81-page-long survey of the extant research on
Megrelian by Harris (1991b) in English.
Megrelian data are also discussed in a number of monographs and articles devoted to
particular domains of linguistic structure in native Georgian linguistics (Asatiani, 1952[2015];
Axalaia, 2004; Č’umburiʒe, 1986; Dadiani, 2014; Margvelašvili, 1983; Mart’irosovi, 1964;
Ǯorbenaʒe, 1991; Žγent’i, 1953), in Russia (Klimov 1965, 1986, 1998b; Klimov & Alekseev
1980) and in the West (Boeder 2005, 2009, 2013; Cherchi 2003; Deeters, 1930; Harris, 1991a,
1993; Hewitt, 1993, 2001, 2004, 2008b; Reseck 2014; Tuite 1998; Vamling & Tchantouria,
1991, 1993, 2005).
Megrelian lexicography has a rich tradition and, unless one counts Güldenstädt’s pilot
word list, begins with a root-based Megrelian-Russian dictionary appended by Q’ipšiʒe to his
(1914) grammar. The next achievement in the field belongs to Čikobava (Chikobava), who
published a comparative Laz-Megrelian-Georgian dictionary in 1938. Further compendia saw
the light of day almost sixty years later (Č’araia, 1997; Eliava, 1997), soon to be followed by
Kaǯaia’s decades-long monumental work finally published in the form of a three-volume
Megrelian-Georgian dictionary (2001–2002), which totals around 29,000 lexical entries
(Rostovtsev-Popiel, 2014, p. 164), abundantly furnished with examples supplied with
translations into Georgian. This dictionary was later amended in several ways: first translated
into German as a Megrelian-German dictionary (Kaǯaia & Fähnrich, 2001), then
complemented with a fourth volume by the author (2009), and finally augmented with the late
Klimov’s Megrelian-Georgian-Russian dictionary co-authored by Kaǯaia and prepared for
publication by Gadilia (2013), which added almost 4,000 new lexical items to the codified
Megrelian lexicon (Rostovtsev-Popiel, 2014, p. 165). Furthermore, one has to mention
Kobalia’s (2010) compendium featuring up to 90,000 entries.

experimental approaches, is largely due to the fundamental works by Żγent’i (1953, 1965)

12.3.2 Suprasegmental Units
The word stress is believed to be “weak dynamic-musical” (K’art’ozia et al., 2011, p. 62) and
can sometimes be distinctive (cf., e.g., dóγuru ‘X died’ versus doγúru(n) ‘X will die’).
However, such minimal pairs are rare. According to Gudava (1969, p. 106), stress typically
falls on the penultimate syllable in bisyllabic words (e.g., k’óči ‘man’) and on the
antepenultimate in trisyllabic words (e.g., ósuri ‘woman’, kóʔope(n) ‘X has apparently been’);
the place of stress in trisyllabics can vary even within the same paradigm depending on the
auslaut: it is the antepenultimate syllable in case the word ends in a vowel (p’č’árundi ‘I
wrote/was writing X’), and the penultimate if the word ends in a consonant (p’č’arúndit ‘we
wrote/were writing X’) (Gudava, 1969, p. 108; see also chapter 16).
Megrelian intonation remains an underexplored area. Similarly to other Kartvelian
languages, Megrelian has a sizable inventory of pitch contours; this has been remarked on
both by speakers and researchers, but has not been studied systematically.16
12.3.3 Phonotactics
Most words in Megrelian are mono-, bi-, and trisyllabic, although longer sequences are also
available (K’art’ozia et al., 2011, p. 61).
Syllables can be open (V, CV),17 closed (VC, CVC), and have either a vocalic (V, VC) or
consonantal onset (CV, CVC). The only major vocalism restriction is that ə is ruled out in the
onset.
With its modest vocalic inventory, as was set forth in Gudava and Gamq’reliʒe’s (1981)
study, Megrelian stands out from the family “in having very strict and extensive principles
governing the internal structure of consonant clusters; while other Kartvelian languages
permit some accessive consonant clusters, Mingrelian permits only decessive clusters (in
which each successive obstruent has a point of articulation further back in the oral cavity”
(Harris, 1991b, pp. 317–318).

12.3.4 Phonological Processes
K’art’ozia et al. (2011) devote substantial space (pp. 62–97) to the description of frequently
occurring phonological processes in Megrelian. These include, but are not limited to, (i)
assimilation and dissimilation (both progressive and, for the most part, (also distant)
regressive, as in gag[i]nilasu > gegnilasu ‘X went mad’); (ii) affricatization (e.g., ǯoγor-s >
ǯoγor-c ‘dog-DAT’ after sonorants, see sections 12.6.1 and 12.7.1) and desaffricatization (e.g.,
c’vanǯ- > c’vand- ‘beard’); (iii) metathesis (e.g., c’ign- > c’ing- ‘book’); (iv) vowel reduction,
which typically affects high vowels and which can be either partial or full (e.g., idu > idǝ > id
‘X went’; gamik’osopes > gamǝk’osopes > gamk’osopes ‘Xs tore Y apart’); and (v) iotacism
(e.g., mu je? < mu re? ‘what’s going on?’ lit. ‘what is X?’).

12.4 Lexical Classes
Lexical classes are determined according to morphological and syntactic criteria.

12.4.1 Verbs
As elsewhere in Kartvelian, an important distinction can be drawn between the finite verb and
the nonfinite verb. The former typically serves as a main predicate and expresses a number of
the categories discussed in sections 12.7.1 and 12.7.2. Nonfinite verb forms include masdars
and participles that syntactically behave like nominals. Masdars are marked for case and
aspect, whereas participles can additionally express number and tense, the latter of which can
be either present, past, or future, whereas the allative case form of the future tense participle
has grammaticalized into the supine (see section 12.7.4).
12.4.2 Nominals
Nominals inflect for case and number, whereby only heads tend to show full inflection.
Adjectives, pronouns, and numerals inflect only when substantivized.
The role of the noun in the expression of spatial semantics is significantly diminished as
compared to Georgian and Svan, as such functions are taken over by the verb with its
sophisticated system of spatial preverbs (Rostovtsev-Popiel, 2008, 2012; see also section
12.7.3).
12.4.3. Inflection-Free Lexical Classes
Inflection-free lexical classes include adverbs, postpositions, conjunctions, particles, and
interjections (see K’art’ozia et al., 2011, pp. 289–309; Kiria et al., 2015, pp. 693–761).
Adverbs can be divided into the following functional groups: locative adverbs (e.g., tudo
‘beneath, alow’), temporal adverbs (e.g., goγ(w)a ‘yesterday’), adverbs of manner (e.g.,
ataš(i) ‘(in) this way’), adverbs of quantity (e.g., etizmaša ‘that many times’), adverbs of
measure (e.g., namt’in(i) ‘excessively’), causal adverbs (e.g., tišen(i) ‘that’s why’), and
interrogative adverbials (e.g., muč’o? ‘how?’).
Adverbs can be either non-derived (e.g., ži(n(i)) ‘up/atop’) or derived (with fossilized
markers of the dative, adverbial, ablative, or allative case), e.g., ška-s ‘between < in the
middle’
, gverd-o[t] ‘by half’, dud-še(n) ‘from the beginning < from the head’, šor-(i)ša ‘far
thither’, respectively). On a specific use of the ergative case marking on adverbs, see example
15 in section 12.8.2.1. Reduplication, either full or partial, is an important means of creating
new adverbs, as in k’ini-k’ini ‘again and again’ (cf. k’in(i) ‘backward, again’), išo-ašo ‘back
and forth’ (lit. ‘thither-hither’).
Postpositions are bound expressions of either nominal or adverbial origin which take a
noun-phrase complement in an oblique case (most typically genitive, either non-truncated or
truncated, i.e., either with š or without it). Postpositions include -c’k’uma/-c’k’ala ‘with’, -
sker(i) ‘like’, -ǯgu(r)a ‘of a kind’, -cal(i) ‘of a kind’, -mang(i) ‘of a kind’, -geša ‘about/in
comparison’, -(ʔ)ure ‘toward’, -šoro ‘like; similar to’. The postposition -x is usually discussed

12.5 Morphology
Megrelian morphology is primarily agglutinative with certain degrees of fusion, including the
root ablaut (specifically, in verbs). Both inflection and derivation employ affixation as the
basic type of morphological exponence, manifesting themselves in suffixation, prefixation,
and circumfixation. In nominal inflection, suffixation is dominant, while verbs actively
employ all three strategies. Verbal infixation is attested as well. However, it typically
represents a result of mophophonological processes rather than purely morphological ones.
For example, in Samurzaq’ano-Zugdidian, the mu part of the root muš- in v-muš-enk ‘I work’
is reanalyzed as the PV mo- (which has an allomorph mu-), and the subject agreement marker
v- migrates to the right of it, consequently undergoes assimilation and turns into p-, which
ultimately results in mu<p>š-enk.

12.6 Nominal Morphology
As was mentioned above, nominals inflect for case and number. Nominals have no category
of grammatical gender. However, animacy can determine adjective derivation and choice of
the verbal lexeme that controls the noun (see section 12.9.2).
12.6.1 Nouns
Nouns distinguish ten cases, four of which are historically based on the genitive (allative,
ablative 1 and 2, and benefactive) (cf. Klimov, 1962, pp. 93–99), and two numbers––singular
and plural.
Noun stems can be consonant- and vowel-final. Nouns with a consonant-final stem often
feature the vowel i appearing between the stem and the case suffix. Some nouns with a vowel-
final stem (typically but not exclusively monosyllabic), such as, e.g., da ‘sister’, insert the
euphonic consonant l between the stem and the plural marker -ep to avoid stem-vowel
contraction (compare NOM.SG cira ‘girl’ and NOM.PL cir-ep-i/*cira-ep-i versus NOM.SG da
‘sister’ and NOM.PL da-l-ep-i/*d-ep-i). In the plural, declension follows the consonant-final
stem pattern.

12.6.4 Pronominals and Deixis
Megrelian distinguishes between proximal and distal deictics; this opposition is primarily
expressed by the vowel alternation featuring e for proximals and i for distals. The latter are
unmarked and therefore likelier to occur in contexts where the referent is not in immediate
proximity to the speaker (Rostovtsev-Popiel, 2009, pp. 24–25).
The inventory of demonstratives is relatively rich due to such derivational means as the
pronominal root t- and the suffix -na, which yield the following items: e versus i, t-e versus t-
i, e-na versus i-na, and t-e-na versus t-i-na, all of these expressions meaning ‘this’ versus
‘that’. The pronouns with -na occur in the substantive function (compare examples 2 and 40),
whereas the ones without -na can be used both substantively and attributively. Compare
examples (37) for the former use and (1b), (14), (15), (40), and (41), showing these pronouns
in the attributive function.
Megrelian also has the vocalic proclitics a= and e=, which can be roughly interpreted as
proximal and distal intensifiers (similar to the particles vot ‘over here’ and von ‘over there’ in
Russian). These elements attach to the left of t- and represent markers of secondary spatial
contrast. The expressions a=t-e and a=t-e-na thus typically refer to items that are closer than
those referred to by regular proximals, whereas the function of e= in distals can vary.
According to my fieldwork data, e=t-i and e=t-i-na are typically used in the Zugdidi region to
point to objects that are located closer to the speaker than those marked by regular distals,
while speakers from Gali, on the contrary, tend to use this emphasis to refer to objects that are
remoter than those marked by regular distals (Rostovtsev-Popiel, 2009, p. 24).

12.6.5 Numerals
Megrelian employs a combined decimal and vigesimal numerical system. 30 The basic
numeral roots, which also function as stems of cardinals, are art- ‘one’, žir- ‘two’, sum-
‘three’, o(n)tx- ‘four’, xut- ‘five’, amšw- ‘six’, škwit- ‘seven’, (b)ruo ‘eight’, čxoro ‘nine’, vit-
‘ten’, eč- ‘twenty’, and oš- ‘hundred’. All other numerals are derived from these expressions.
Cardinals from 11 to 19 are formed according to the model vit- ‘ten’ + -[d]o- ‘and’ + one
of the basic elements from ‘one’ to ‘nine’, e.g., vit-o-žir- ‘12’, vit-o-bruo ‘18’. To form a
cardinal from 20 to 29, Megrelian uses the model eč- ‘20’ + -do- ‘and’ + one of the basics,
e.g., eč-do-art- ‘21’, eč-do-amšw- ‘26’. The formation of items from 30 to 39 employs the
model eč- ‘20’ + -do- ‘and’ + ‘10’ + one of the basics, e.g., eč-do-vit- ‘30’, eč-do-vit-o-xut-
‘35’, eč-do-vit-o-čxoro ‘39’. A complex prefix is added to create higher numerals, compare
eč-do-škvit- ‘27’, žir-n-eč-do-škvit- ‘47’ (lit. ‘two times twenty and seven’), sum-on-eč-do-

from the basic set, such as otx-oš- ‘400’. To say ‘one thousand’, one would use either the
Megrelian word vit-oš- (lit. ‘10 times hundred’) or the one adapted from Georgian, viz.

12.7 Verbal Morphology
The following categories are expressed by the Megrelian finite verb: tense, aspect, mood, and
evidentiality,31 person, and number. Compared to the other languages in the Kartvelian
family, Megrelian features a greater number of the TAME paradigms, has a lower number of
suppletive verbal stems, and provides for a particularly rich set of prefixal derivations.
Valency alternations in Megrelian are quite elaborate. Megrelian has active
monotransitives and ditransitives, intransitives, decausatives (with passive readings available),
and inverted statives (see section 12.8.2.1). Nearly all verbs can undergo causativization
(double causatives also being possible) and applicativization (see section 12.8.2.3).
31 These are abbreviated as TAME in this chapter, in contrast to the traditional tripartite term TAM.

12.7.4. Non-finite Verb Forms
Nonfinite verb forms include masdars, participles, and supines.

process/way of writing’). Their function is close to that of the infinitive. Masdars express the
category of aspect and inflect for case. Numberwise they are always singular.
Masdar formation from a stem involves suffixation, the actual array of affixes being wide
and often requiring an ad hoc explanation, although it has been possible to make certain
generalizations in this respect.38

12.8.2 Simple Clauses
Two essential morphological and morphosyntactic features that are most salient for the
structure of the simple clause in Megrelian are verb class and case marking.
12.8.2.1 Verb Classes
Megrelian, similarly to its sister languages, features four verb classes, whose distribution rests
upon “morphological and/or morphosyntactic criteria” (Cherchi, 2003, p. 30). These are: (i)
active dynamic verbs, typically bi- and trivalent transitives (e.g., write, eat), (ii) intransitives
with patientive semantics (e.g., hide, rot) and their applicatives (e.g., hide from X, rot at X’s
place), (iii) intransitives with agentive semantics (e.g., sing, play), and (iv) inverted statives
that are typically bivalent (e.g., love, hate). Classes I and III case-shift entirely, and Class II as
well, see Table 12.19, whereas Class IV verbs case-shift only partially, thus only affecting the
case of the second argument (i.e., the theme/stimulus object that becomes a formal subject of
particular TAMEs):

12.8.3 Complex Sentences
Complex sentences include complex sentences proper and sentences with quoted or reported
speech. The former are introduced either by =t(i) or =n(i)/=i, whereby additional
complementizers, such as nam(u)da ‘that’, are available as well. Section 12.8.3.3 adresses
reported speech constructions.

12.9 Lexicon
A large number of lexical items go back to Common Kartvelian, Georgian-Zan, and
Megrelian-Laz strata. This original lexical inventory has been enriched by borrowings from
Georgian, Russian, and, to some extent, Abkhaz. All other lexical borrowing layers can hardly
be viewed as significant.
12.9.1 Phonosemantic Component in the Lexicon
Megrelian possesses dozens of (either entirely or partially reduplicated) onomatopoetic items
that constitute, in Klimov’s words, “gradual systems” within the semantic clusters they group
in (compare, e.g., cki-ckon-u-a ‘pluck off lightly’, c’k’i-c’k’on-u-a ‘nip off squeamishly while
eating’, ʒgi-ʒgon-u-a ‘pluck off bigger pieces’, ǯgi-ǯgon-u-a ‘tear off brutally’) (Klimov,
1986, p. 77).
In their recent study on phonosemantics, Gersamia et al. (2016, pp. 63, 449), with a
reference to Kobalava (1979), point out that it is unrealistic to aim at providing an exhaustive
list of such clusters, making it impossible to count the resulting lexical items as such, for most
of those are typically produced ad hoc in discourse. According to Gudava (1958) and K’iziria
(1982) apud Gersamia et al. (2016, pp. 130–131; 461–462), the latter page numbers referring
to an ample summary in English), consonant phonosemantics in Megrelian stems––to a large
extent similarly to the rest of Kartvelian––can be represented as shown in Table 12.20:

The hissing consonants tend to be associated with small size and lightness, whereas the

The vocalic sound symbolism features a basic opposition of a and i, the former standing

with something small, narrow, upward, rounded, and/or bent.

The parameter of animacy (see section 12.6) affects the classification of adjectives.

modify.46 In such instances, inanimate-modifying adjectives are derived by the suffix -ur/-ul
